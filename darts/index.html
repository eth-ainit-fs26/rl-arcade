    One move, every round. Let's write it as a single line.

    aim += α × error

    The Robbins–Monro update rule.

    Here's that nudge written as one line.

    y(t+1) = y(t) + α(t) × error

    • y(t) — your aim at timestep t.
    • error — how far off the shot was, and in which direction (signed).
    • α(t) — the learning rate at timestep t, decaying over time.

    This rule is Robbins–Monro — the root of stochastic approximation. Let's implement it.

    Why does the rate shrink?

    Why does the learning rate α(t) get smaller as the rounds go on?

    Early on you're likely far from the bullseye, so a large step closes the gap fast. As you get close, a large step would fly straight past the target and bounce back. Shrinking α(t) lets the aim fine-tune and converge — this decaying schedule is exactly the Robbins–Monro condition for convergence.

    What if α never decayed?

    Suppose you kept α large and constant forever instead of letting it decay. What happens?

    With a fixed, large step size, every shot's error sends the aim lurching past the bullseye and back — it orbits the target forever without settling, and the noise makes it worse. Letting α(t) decay toward zero is precisely what guarantees the aim converges.
