Think recursively.

Don't ask "what is the best whole path?".
Ask a smaller question about one room:

The key question

"What is the maximum spookiness I can still collect, starting from room at (row, col)?"

The answer is the room spookiness, plus the best I can do from whichever neighbour I step into next.

=

The Bellman equation.

Let V(row, col) be the maximum spookiness obtainable from that room to the exit.
Then every room obeys one beautiful relationship:

V(row, col) = spookiness(row, col) + max( V(Down) , V(Right) )

  • spookiness(row, col) — the points for the room you are standing in right now.
  • V(Down) — the best spookiness you can still collect if you step into the room directly below.
  • V(Right) — the best spookiness you can still collect if you step into the room to the right.

This famous equation rules reinforcement learning, optimal control and economics . Let's implement it.

What is the base case?

What does compute_max_spookiness return when it reaches the exit room?

At the exit there is no "right" or "down" to move into, so the recursion stops. The most spookiness you can still collect from there is simply the exit room's own value — that's the base case that anchors the whole recursion.

Why does brute force fail?

Why don't we just enumerate every path and pick the best?

A 5×5 grid already has 70 right/down paths, and an 8×8 grid has 3 432 — far too many to trace one by one, and the count keeps exploding as the grid grows. The Bellman recursion instead asks one small question per room — the spookiness here plus the best of its two neighbours — so it never has to walk a single full path.
